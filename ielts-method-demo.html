The forgotten role of women in medieval arts
A A team of archaeologists recently and unexpectedly revealed direct archaeological evidence of the involvement of medieval women in the production of manuscripts. This challenges the widespread assumptions that men were the sole producers of books throughout the Middle Ages (600–1500 AD). They did so by identifying particles of blue pigment in the fossilised dental plaque of a medieval woman as lapis lazuli, an extremely valuable stone at the time. The findings strongly suggest it will be possible to increase the profile of ancient female artists in the historical and archaeological record by analysing their dirty teeth. Q1
B This discovery was made possible by applying technological advances to a little-studied deposit on teeth known as tartar, which is mineralised dental plaque. Plaque built up and mineralised over the course of people's lives. When dental plaque hardens and becomes tartar, it can entomb these particles and molecules for hundreds or thousands of years – potentially even millions. Q6
C The majority of scholarly work on ancient tartar has been centred on what people ate but, besides food, the human mouth is subject to a constant influx of particles of different types from the environment. Tree and grass pollen, spores, cotton fibres, medicinal plants and micro-charcoal have all been reported among finds from this type of dental analysis. Q8 Despite such promising evidence, the value of tartar as environmental evidence has not, so far, been much exploited. Q2
D The team analysed the skeletal remains of a female individual, known as B78, who lived in the 11th–12th century. Her skeletal remains had not indicated anything particular about her life, besides a general indication that she probably did not do any hard labour. Q3 The blue particles were unlike any other discovery – firstly because of their colour, and secondly because of their sheer number. Q7
E To be sure about the blue powder trapped in the woman's tartar, a range of microscopy techniques were used. The blue specks were lazurite, the blue portion of the lapis lazuli stone, a substance more precious than gold in Medieval Europe. Q10 Afghanistan was the only source, and the preparation of the pigment took great skill. Q11
F A variety of reasons were possible, from painting to accidental ingestion, or even the consumption of the powder as a medicine. Q9 The way the blue particles were found – single flecks in different areas – pointed to repeated exposure, not a single intake. This suggests it's more likely that this ultramarine pigment was brought into the region as a finished product for use by artists. Q4
G The most likely explanation: this was an artist who repeatedly used her lips to make a fine point on the end of her brush in order to paint intricate detail on manuscripts.
